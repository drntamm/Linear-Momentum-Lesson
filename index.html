Core Concepts: Momentum and Impulse

This section covers the fundamental definitions of linear momentum and impulse, and the critical relationship that connects them through force and time.

1. Linear Momentum (p)

Momentum is a measure of the **mass in motion**. It's a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

p = m · v
  • m: mass (in kg)
  • v: velocity (in m/s)
  • Units: kg·m/s

Worked Example 1: Calculating Momentum

A 0.15 kg baseball is thrown with a velocity of 40 m/s eastward. What is its momentum?

  • Given: m = 0.15 kg, v = 40 m/s (East)
  • Formula: p = m · v
  • Solution: p = (0.15 kg)(40 m/s) = 6.0 kg·m/s
  • Answer: 6.0 kg·m/s eastward.

2. Impulse (J)

Impulse is the product of the average net force and the time interval over which it acts. Impulse is what **causes** a change in momentum.

J = Favg · Δt
  • Favg: average net force (in Newtons, N)
  • Δt: time interval (in seconds, s)
  • Units: N·s (which is identical to kg·m/s)

3. The Impulse-Momentum Theorem

By combining Newton's Second Law with the definitions of momentum and impulse, we get the most important relationship in this chapter:

J = Favg · Δt = Δp = pfinal - pinitial

The impulse applied to an object equals its change in momentum.

Worked Example 2: Impulse & Force

A 5.0 kg bowling ball moving at +2.0 m/s hits a cushion and bounces back at -1.5 m/s. The impact lasts 0.05 s.

A: Calculate the Impulse (J)

  • pi = (5.0 kg)(+2.0 m/s) = +10.0 kg·m/s
  • pf = (5.0 kg)(-1.5 m/s) = -7.5 kg·m/s
  • J = pf - pi = (-7.5) - (+10.0) = -17.5 N·s

B: Calculate the Average Force (Favg)

  • Favg = J / Δt
  • Favg = (-17.5 N·s) / (0.05 s) = -350 N
  • Answer: The cushion exerted a 350 N force in the negative direction.

The Law of Conservation of Momentum

This section explores one of the most fundamental laws in physics: in an isolated system, the total momentum never changes. This principle explains everything from rifle recoil to rocket propulsion.

1. The Law

The total linear momentum of an **isolated system** remains constant.

  • An "isolated system" means no net external forces (like friction) are acting on the objects.
  • The total momentum *before* an interaction (like a collision or explosion) equals the total momentum *after*.
Pinitial = Pfinal

For a two-object system:

m1v1i + m2v2i = m1v1f + m2v2f

Reminder: Velocity (v) is a vector! Use positive (+) and negative (-) signs to indicate direction.

2. Example: Rifle Recoil

A rifle and a bullet form a system. Before firing, everything is at rest, so the total momentum is zero.

Initial State (Pi = 0)

Rifle (mR) + Bullet (mB) at v = 0

Final State (Pf = 0)

←
Rifle Recoil
(-pR)
→
Bullet Fires
(+pB)

To keep the total momentum zero, the backward momentum of the rifle must perfectly cancel the forward momentum of the bullet.

0 = pRifle + pBullet  ⇒  pRifle = -pBullet

Their momentum magnitudes are equal!

Worked Example 3: Inelastic Explosion/Recoil

A 60 kg student is at rest on frictionless ice. They push a 2.0 kg medicine ball, giving it a velocity of +5.0 m/s. What is the student's recoil velocity?

  • System: Student (s) + Ball (b)
  • Initial Momentum (Pi): msvsi + mbvbi = (60)(0) + (2.0)(0) = 0
  • Final Momentum (Pf): msvsf + mbvbf = (60)vsf + (2.0)(+5.0)
  • Conservation: Pi = Pf
  • Solve: 0 = 60vsf + 10.0
  • 60vsf = -10.0
  • vsf = -10.0 / 60 ≈ -0.167 m/s
  • Answer: The student recoils at 0.167 m/s in the opposite direction.

Types of Collisions

Collisions are where momentum conservation becomes most powerful. We categorize them based on whether kinetic energy (the energy of motion) is also conserved.

Collision Types Overview

Type of Collision Momentum (p) Kinetic Energy (K) Defining Feature
Perfectly Elastic Conserved Conserved Objects bounce perfectly. $K_f = K_i$.
Inelastic Conserved NOT Conserved Objects deform; energy is lost as heat/sound. $K_f < K_i$.
Perfectly Inelastic Conserved NOT Conserved Objects **stick together** after collision.

1. Perfectly Inelastic Collision

This is the easiest to solve. The objects merge and move with a **single final velocity (vf)**.

The conservation equation simplifies:

m1v1i + m2v2i = (m1 + m2)vf

Maximum kinetic energy is lost in this type of collision.

Worked Example 4: The Sticky Collision

A 1000 kg car (m1) at 20 m/s (v1i) hits a stationary 1500 kg truck (m2). They lock bumpers.

  • Formula: m1v1i + m2v2i = (m1 + m2)vf
  • Solve: (1000)(20) + (1500)(0) = (1000 + 1500)vf
  • 20,000 = 2500 vf
  • vf = 20,000 / 2500 = 8.0 m/s
  • Answer: The wreckage moves at 8.0 m/s.

2. Perfectly Elastic Collision

The "ideal" collision (like billiard balls). Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.

This gives you a system of **two** equations to solve, which is more complex:

1. Momentum Conservation:

m1v1i + m2v2i = m1v1f + m2v2f

2. Kinetic Energy Conservation:

½m1v1i2 + ½m2v2i2 = ½m1v1f2 + ½m2v2f2

You must use both equations to solve for the two unknown final velocities (v1f and v2f).

Angular Momentum

Momentum also applies to rotating objects. Angular momentum is the rotational equivalent of linear momentum and is also a conserved quantity.

1. Defining Angular Momentum (L)

Angular momentum measures an object's tendency to continue rotating. For a simple point mass $m$ moving at speed $v$ in a circle of radius $r$:

L = mvr

The more general equation involves Moment of Inertia ($I$) and angular velocity ($\omega$):

L = I · ω

2. Conservation of Angular Momentum

Angular momentum is conserved if there is no net external **torque** (a rotational force).

Example: Ice Skater

Arms Out

Large Radius (r)
Large Inertia (I)
↓
Slow Spin (ω)

Arms In

Small Radius (r)
Small Inertia (I)
↓
Fast Spin (ω)

By pulling her arms in, the skater decreases her inertia ($I$). To keep $L$ ($= I\omega$) constant, her angular velocity ($\omega$) must increase, and she spins faster.

Practice Zone

Test your knowledge with these problems from the lesson.

1. Conceptual Review

When you fire a bullet from a rifle, is the magnitude of the rifle's momentum larger, smaller, or the same as the bullet's momentum?

Show Answer

Answer: The same. The total momentum of the rifle-bullet system (which was zero) must remain zero. This means the individual momenta must be equal and opposite (prifle = −pbullet). Their magnitudes are identical.

2. Impulse and Velocity Change

An average net force of 50 N is applied to a 0.05 kg tennis ball for 0.02 s.

  1. Calculate the impulse (J).
  2. What is the change in the ball's velocity (Δ v)?
Show Answer

1. Impulse:
J = F · Δt = (50 N)(0.02 s) = 1.0 N·s

2. Velocity Change:
Δp = J = m · Δv
Δv = J / m = (1.0 N·s) / (0.05 kg) = 20 m/s

3. Perfectly Inelastic Collision

A 90 kg running back (m1) moving at 6.0 m/s (v1i) tackles a stationary 120 kg defensive lineman (m2). If they stick together, what is their final speed?

Show Answer

Formula: m1v1i + m2v2i = (m1 + m2)vf

Solution:
(90)(6.0) + (120)(0) = (90 + 120) vf
540 = (210) vf
vf = 540 / 210 ≈ 2.57 m/s
